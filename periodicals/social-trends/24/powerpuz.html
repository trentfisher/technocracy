Power Puzzle

Ron Miller

1985

Published in:
  • Section 3 Newsletter, Aug. 1985, No. 24

Forbes Magazine, the highly conservative business publication, stated on the cover of its February 1985 issue that ``The failure of the U.S. nuclear power program ranks as the largest managerial disaster in business history, a disaster on a monumental scale. The utility industry has already invested $125 billion in nuclear power, with an additional $140 billion to come before the decade is out, and only the blind, or the biased, can now think that most of the money has been well spent. It is a defeat for the U.S. consumer and for the competitiveness of U.S. industry, for the utilities that undertook the program and for the private enterprise system that made it possible.''

The nuclear power industry provides an excellent example of the many pitfalls that exist within our highly competitive capitalistic Price System. Many nuclear plants were built on what is referred to as fast-track construction, which means that design errors were intentionally left for others to correct; very little checking of vendor quality was done. Regulations affecting basic design criteria were subject to frequent shifts. Construction frequently proceeded without any planning. Under such circumstances it is surprising that problems similar to those at Three Mile Island have not occurred with great frequency.

The inept management of nuclear power has effectively eliminated it as an option for expanding our supply of electrical energy. Yet every year our need for power increases. With proper research and planning it is possible to increase our supply of electricity and reduce our dependency on energy outside North America by making use of technology available to us now.

Many years ago Technocracy Inc. made a design proposal that rated far more serious attention than it received then or since. In terms of implementation, the first step would have to be the installation of a high voltage D.C. transmission line linking the entire North American Continent into a single electrical network. Direct current cannot be transformed to higher or lower voltages; therefore it is necessary to transform the power to be transmitted up to the proper voltage with alternating current then convert the power to direct current for transmission. This means that large and expensive substations must be constructed. What, then, is the gain? With the same size conductor, about twice the amount of power can be transmitted.

High voltage alternating current also generates many unfavorable side effects. Capacitance and inductance effects with the ground, and other conductors nearby are created that must be allowed for in the design. Line losses due to radiation are significant. The 1,000,000 volt direct current transmission line proposed by Technocracy Inc. has no equal even today. It would be a coaxial bipolar conductor with one pole running down the center, separated from the second pole, in the form of a pipe, by spacer rings.

Surrounding this outer pole would be another, larger diameter pipe to contain both poles. Technocracy's original proposal called for nitrogen gas as an insulator between the two poles. Today sulfur hexaflouride would most likely be used. The two concentric outer pipes would use some type of dielectric oil to protect against leaks. The line would be mounted in an underground concrete vault.

When Technocracy first made this proposal, such a thing had never been heard of. Many were certain it couldn't be done and that the cost would be prohibitive. Today, for the most part, the technology required for such a line exists in gas insulated substations. The cost and size of D.C. substations are dropping, and a little more research could contribute to a further decline in cost.

Such a line would reduce the right-of-way from about 150 feet down to about 8 feet. The conductor would be underground and not nearly so vulnerable to destruction by man or nature. Due to rising power demands, right-of-way corridors are becoming crowded with power lines. There are no fewer than 14 major transmission lines leading onto and out of Grand Coulee Dam. One of the major right-of-way corridors connecting California to the Northwest has seven transmission lines. Maintenance on such an underground D.C. transmission system would be almost negligible.

Presently most utilities require 20% to 30% over their base load generating capacity to service their peak load. This additional capacity could be reduced with the ability to pass the peak loads from time zone to time zone as the peak loads travel with the sun. It would then become possible to generate power in the most efficient way and at the least cost. Hydropower sites far removed from the major population centers could be developed. The hydropower of Northern Canada, for instance, could be sent anywhere it was needed.

As the second part of such a design, Technocracy proposed the installation of a Continental Hydrology. This would involve the damming of major North American rivers, which would provide much more needed hydropower plus a canal system for transporting goods at low cost, and it would slow down the rivers, adding to their size; also, it would strengthen the water tables and help conserve our fresh water.

Hydroelectricity is the safest, cleanest, and cheapest source of electric energy available, present or future. The installation of a D.C. transmission grid and a Continental Hydrology on North America would act to reduce the cost of energy for all by making available the cheapest form of electrical generation. By interconnecting the entire Continent with this type of grid, a minimum of maintenance would be required.

Nuclear power (particularly as executed under our present Price System) is not only risky, it produces an even more deadly waste product about which debate still rages. What do we do with it? Fuel cells are now beginning to be produced in power-plant size that operate in near silence at efficiencies far higher than thermal plants, and their primary waste products are oxygen and heat. Fuel cells can be produced on an assembly line basis then connected together on the site to make the largest station required for the area, thus drastically reducing the lead time required for construction. More research into fluidized bed combustion of coal could result in large reductions in objectionable effluents and increase efficiencies as well. Although a few years from full development, thermonuclear power promises to be far cleaner and much safer than nuclear power. Solar power is still under development, but the research is producing good results.
