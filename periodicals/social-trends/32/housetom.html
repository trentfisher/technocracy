House Of Tomorrow

Lois M. Scheel

1986

Published in:
  • Section 3 Newsletter, April 1986, No. 32

For several decades we've had the technological expertise and sufficient resources to design a house of tomorrow that is: (1) durable enough to last the maximum time possible with a minimum of physical maintenance cost; (2) fireproof and strong enough to withstand every normal stress and strain but light enough to be easily handled; (3) abundant and cheap enough from an energy standpoint to house the entire population of the Continent without straining natural resource supplies.

In 1938 Technocracy gave us a glimpse of a home of the future. Wood as a building material was not durable enough and required too much human labor in construction to justify its use. Bricks and stone were too heavy to handle and likewise required too much human labor in constructing a house, not to mention creating a serious earthquake menace with their lack of flexibility. Chromate, copper and nickel failed to pass resource requirements as there weren't enough of them in the world to house a small part of the 150,000,000 people then living in North America.

At that time only three or four materials filled the necessary requirements for Continental housing from a technological standpoint. Glass came first. It was made chiefly from silicon, the second most abundant element in the earth's crust. It filled the requirements of strength and durability and could be fabricated in the form of glass wool or bubble-glass, both of which were light weight and provided almost perfect insulation against heat and sound. The housing industry could have turned out almost unlimited quantities of strong, light-weight, flexible, durable, honeycomb glass panels that would have been an ideal construction material.

Technocracy proposed concrete as a second possible material, not as it was known then but in the form of a thin, honeycomb panel of solid concrete filled with a recently developed concrete "foam", which was soundproof and heatproof. Such panels would be strong, yet lightweight and easily handled and could be cheaply turned out by mass-production methods, answering the requirement for an abundance of material sufficient to rehouse the entire continent.

The third possible material suggested was aluminum because it was one of the three most abundant elements in the earth's crust and also filled the requirements of strength, durability and lightness. It's only drawback at that time was the energy cost of producing the metal that undoubtedly "could have been overcome by the development of mass-production methods."

Technocracy's proposal for the fourth possible material of the "house of tomorrow" was some form of 20th Century "plastics." Plastics could be obtained from wood and

waste farm products, both of them replaceable resources. Plastic materials had possibilities that were only just then becoming recognized. The energy cost of production was rather high and there were several other drawbacks at that time to using plastics as a building material but, as Technocracy pointed out in 1938, "with the development of mass-production techniques--this material may eventually supplant all other materials derived from non-replaceable resources." Today we can see how far the use of plastics has advanced! In some instances plastic is a threat to our environment because of its durability (disposable bottles, disposable diapers, etc.), but handled intelligently it could be one of our most valuable replaceable resources due to its efficiency, durability and low-energy costs.

Undoubtedly other efficient housing materials have been brought to light through scientific research, but model houses featured then and now are nothing more than glorified salesrooms that deplete nonrenewable natural resources unnecessarily, promote cheap gadgetry instead of energy efficiency, and seldom offer real comfort or convenience. For those who have the money, showmanship is the name of the game. For those who must settle for mediocrity, the house begins to fall apart before the hapless owner can realize the first payment on the principle. Yet, up until now the value of even a mediocre house tended to increase through the years that made it a good investment, especially for the middle class.

However, the time when the value of a house appreciates while the structure actually depreciates is fading away. The American Dream for many homeowners has become a nightmare. Although education systems and parents still try to instill the value of hard work and owning a home in the young, the children of today weren't born into an era of "good times" brought about by a World War II. They are facing a challenge many of their parents haven't come to terms with yet: the technological age where the efficient kilowatt hour replaces man hours. With jobs on the decline, where will purchasing power come from? For adults to blame the youth of today for drug abuse, drinking, lack of interest, crime increase, and escalating suicide rates without seeing themselves in the cataclysmic picture as well reveals their extremely selfish bias toward keeping the events of yesterday the same. They want their children to toil because they had to toil. They cannot tolerate the thought of them living in a good house without having to slave away 45 years of their lives for it, perhaps then to realize it really doesn't belong to them after all. Many people can't hang onto these homes on retirement income because of high taxes and the high cost of maintaining a house.

All these years we could have provided adequate housing for all North Americans without it being a burden to them. Ask political leaders from Franklin Roosevelt's time on why they chose not to share North America's abundance of natural resources with all citizens as their birthright. This phenomenon continues today while people bed down in the streets and under bridges. We, the North American citizens, are allowing it to happen as we resign ourselves to the social attrition perpetuated by our leaders who care more for profits than energy conservation and the welfare of their country's citizens.
