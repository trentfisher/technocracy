Wartime Economy

Lois M. Scheel

1985

Published in:
  • Section 3 Newsletter, March 1985, No. 19
Our present society is heading toward a fascist social system as our wartime economy indicates. Many short-sighted persons believe that war is good for the economy. War pulls nations out of depressions. They don't take into consideration that after the war is over, civilizations are thrown back into more severe recessions as new technology, developed to help win the war (if wars are ever won) continues to be used in peacetime conditions. War proves as nothing else does our ability to develop new technology quickly for travel expediency, precise death weapons, instant communication systems, etc. So profitable has the war industry become for some people that these beneficiaries of war economics want to keep a good thing going.

Washington State is one of the military oases of the nation. Washington State employs more military personnel than almost any other part of the nation. In Puget Sound, military preparation and production are conducted on land, on the sea, and beneath the sea, assembling the mightiest war machine on earth. In Bangor, WA is the Trident Submarine Base. At the University of Washington 100 engineers inquire daily into subtleties of undersea warfare. The late Senator "Scoop" Jackson was known in Washington D.C. as the "senator from Boeing", the sixth-largest defense contractor. There is the cruise missile lab in Kent and Lockheed Shipyards in Seattle. Then there is the "nuclear culture" residing in Hanford where the football players proudly display mushroom clouds on their helmets and where streets and places of business, entertainment and education are named after nuclear weapons and their consequences. There isn't room here to cover all the establishments that touch on war profit in the state of Washington.

An effort to stampede the country into war in 1916 by the Wilson administration brought this comment from a minister in a New England munitions town: "The war is a godsend to this community." (From Revolution in Seattle, Harvey O'Connor.

Shortly after the U.S. detonated the first H-bomb in 1954, News and World Report in a state of euphoria glorified the H-bomb as a boon to business "a long period...of big orders. In the years ahead the affects of the new bomb will keep on increasing. As one appraiser puts it: 'The H-bomb has blown Depression thinking right out the window.'"

The short-sightedness of this type of thinking should be cause for alarm in every conscious citizen. Hitler pulled Germany out of a depression with his military buildup, but what good is a product if you don't use it? So these mindless leaders create reasons for war. And once more Germany is facing a serious depression. In fact, one out of every 10 Europeans is out of work. Living standards are dropping for the first time in 30 years.

In the five years ending in 1986, taxpayers will contribute a healthy share toward a $1.5 trillion military buildup. Starting in the year 2100 B.C. and spending $1 million every day right up to this moment, you still will not have spent as much as the United States is slated to spend on armaments between 1981 and 1986 A.D.

Seth Neddermeyer, a former Manhattan Project scientist told Dean Paton, Seattle Post Intelligencer, on May 1, 1983, that "possibilities for world survival are limited because corruption of research by the military pervades all branches of science." He further stated that "Our foreign policy is like a three-year-old kid; aggressive as hell. We're not learning from the Holocaust, from the bombings of the Japanese, from anything. We're still in a wartime economy."

Definition of fascism according to Webster's 1983 New Twentieth Century Dictionary: A system of government characterized by rigid one-party dictatorship, forcible suppression of the opposition (unions, other parties, especially leftist, minority groups, etc.), the retention of private ownership of the means of production under centralized governmental control, belligerent nationalism and racism, and glorification of war.

..... As a nation, how near are we to fitting this definition?
